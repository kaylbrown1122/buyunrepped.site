Knoxville has quietly become one of Tennessee's most competitive housing markets. The University of Tennessee, Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and a wave of remote workers drawn by the Smoky Mountains have all pushed demand well above what local supply can meet. The median home price in Knox County sits around $301,000, and with buyer's agent commissions averaging 2.42%, the typical Knoxville buyer is paying $7,284 for representation they may not need.<sup>1</sup><sup>2</sup>

This guide is for Knoxville buyers who want to keep that money. Going unrepresented in Knoxville is more than viable. It requires knowing the market, understanding Knox County's specific closing cost structure, and building the right lean support team. Here's how to do all of it.

## Knoxville's Housing Market in 2026

Knoxville's market has cooled from its 2021-2022 peak but remains meaningfully more competitive than most Tennessee markets. Inventory is tight in desirable inner-city neighborhoods, and properties in the $250,000–$400,000 range still move relatively quickly.

Key current conditions:<sup>1</sup><sup>3</sup>

- Median sale price in Knox County: approximately $301,000
- Days on market: 30–60 days on average, with faster movement in popular neighborhoods
- Inventory: improving but still below historical norms in inner Knoxville
- New construction: active in west and northwest Knox County, adding supply in the suburbs

The buyers best positioned to go unrepresented in Knoxville are those who know the neighborhoods they want, have done their pricing research, and can move decisively when the right property appears. In the most competitive neighborhoods, hesitation costs more than the commission you're saving.

## Knoxville Neighborhoods: A Buyer's Orientation

Knoxville's neighborhoods vary significantly in character, price, and inventory. Here's the orientation buyers doing their own research need.

### Inner Knoxville

- **4th and Gill**: One of Knoxville's most active historic districts. Victorian homes, bungalows, strong neighborhood association. Prices $250,000–$500,000, with renovated homes at the top end.
- **Old City / Downtown**: Lofts, condos, and mixed-use buildings near downtown. Walkable, urban, limited single-family inventory.
- **Fort Sanders**: University-adjacent neighborhood, mix of student housing and owner-occupied historic homes. Investment activity is high. Buyers should verify rental-permitted zoning if they're not buying purely for owner-occupancy.
- **Parkridge**: Emerging neighborhood east of downtown. Active renovation market, more affordable than 4th and Gill, with similar bones.
- **Holston Hills**: East Knoxville, established neighborhood with river views, larger lots, 1940s–1960s housing stock. Prices $250,000–$450,000.

### Central and West Knoxville

- **Bearden / West Hills**: Established, suburban character west of downtown. Good schools, walkable commercial areas, mix of mid-century and newer construction. Prices $300,000–$500,000.
- **Sequoyah Hills**: Premium west Knoxville neighborhood along the Tennessee River. Established, larger lots, significant price premium. Entry-level starts around $500,000.
- **Farragut**: Technically a separate municipality within Knox County. Highly rated schools, strong demand, newer construction. Prices typically $400,000–$700,000.

### North and South Knoxville

- **North Knoxville / Fountain City**: More affordable than west or inner Knoxville. Established neighborhoods, larger lots, longer commutes to UT and ORNL. Prices $200,000–$350,000.
- **South Knoxville**: Rapidly revitalizing area south of the river. Proximity to Urban Wilderness trail system driving price appreciation. Entry-level homes still under $300,000 in some pockets, but prices are rising.

### Knox County Suburbs

- **Powell / Halls**: North Knox County. Residential, affordable, growing. Prices $220,000–$380,000.
- **Karns**: Northwest Knox County. Similar character to Powell, newer development, expanding commercial base.
- **Hardin Valley**: West of Farragut, significant new construction, strong school district. Growing tech worker population near ORNL corridor. Prices $350,000–$600,000.

## Step 1: Build Your Support Team

### Hire a Knoxville Real Estate Attorney

Tennessee doesn't require an attorney to close, but for unrepresented buyers, this is your most important hire.<sup>5</sup> Knoxville real estate attorneys typically charge $600–$1,200 for standard residential closing representation. For more complex situations involving older properties, estates, or unclear titles, hourly rates run $150–$300.

The University of Tennessee College of Law maintains a list of area attorneys, and the Knox County Bar Association has a referral service. Both are reasonable starting points for finding a qualified real estate attorney.

### Get Pre-Approved With a Lender

In Knoxville's more competitive neighborhoods, a strong pre-approval letter matters. Sellers and listing agents take offers more seriously when financing is clearly in order. Get quotes from at least three lenders: at least one local bank or credit union, and one or two online lenders for comparison.

Ask each lender about [seller concession limits](/resources/seller-concessions-tennessee-guide) for your loan type. FHA allows 6%, conventional loans 3%–6% depending on down payment. In Knoxville's market, seller concession requests are common and generally accepted outside of multiple-offer situations.

### Line Up a Home Inspector

Knoxville inspections typically run $325–$425 for a standard home. Book early, especially in spring and summer when wait times stretch to 10–14 days in a busy market.

Knoxville-specific inspection concerns:

- **Radon**: Knox County is in an EPA Zone 1 radon area, the highest-risk designation. Radon testing should be included with every inspection. If levels are above 4 pCi/L, mitigation runs $800–$2,500. This is a significant and often overlooked Knoxville-specific issue.<sup>6</sup>
- **Foundation and grading**: East Tennessee's hilly terrain creates drainage challenges. Crawlspace moisture and grading issues are common in older Knox County neighborhoods.
- **Septic systems**: Properties outside Knoxville's sewer service area use septic systems. Have any septic system inspected separately, $150–$350, and verify the location and size of the tank and field lines.
- **HVAC age**: Cold winters and humid summers stress equipment. Systems older than 12 years should be evaluated.

Our post on [Tennessee home inspection issues that derail deals](/resources/tennessee-home-inspection-issues) covers the most common findings across the state.

## Step 2: Search Knoxville's Market

Zillow, Redfin, and Realtor.com display the Knoxville MLS in near-real time. For inner-city neighborhoods (4th and Gill, Parkridge, South Knoxville), new listings move quickly. Set up alerts and be prepared to see properties within 1–2 days of listing.

Open houses are well-attended in popular Knoxville neighborhoods. Attend without signing any buyer-broker agreement. For private showings, contact the listing agent directly and identify yourself as an unrepresented buyer. Tennessee law (TCA 62-13-405) requires the listing agent to disclose their role verbally and confirm it in writing before preparing any offer.<sup>7</sup>

If you're targeting Farragut or Knox County suburbs with significant new construction, contact builders directly. New construction purchases don't always require a buyer's agent, and the savings on a $400,000+ new build are substantial.

## Step 3: Research What Knoxville Homes Are Actually Worth

Knox County's geographic variation means comps require careful attention to location. A home on the river in Sequoyah Hills and a comparable-sized home two miles inland can differ dramatically in value.

Use the Knox County Property Assessor's database for tax assessment history and prior sale records.<sup>8</sup> Pair this with Redfin's closed sales data. Focus on:

- Homes that closed within the past 6 months
- Within 0.5 miles of your target property
- Within 15–20% of the square footage
- Comparable lot size and condition

In active renovation areas like South Knoxville and Parkridge, be careful about using unrenovated comps to evaluate renovated homes and vice versa. The spread between distressed and updated can be $100/square foot or more.

## Step 4: Make an Offer in Knoxville

### Strategy by Neighborhood

Knoxville's market isn't uniform. Your approach should match the neighborhood:

- **High-demand inner neighborhoods** (4th and Gill, Sequoyah Hills, Farragut schools): Move quickly, offer at or near list on well-priced homes, keep contingencies but minimize contingency periods.
- **Suburban and outlying areas** (Powell, Halls, North Knox): More room to negotiate. Homes sit longer. Offers 2%–4% below list are reasonable starting points on properties that have been on market 30+ days.
- **New construction**: Negotiate on upgrades, rate buydowns, and closing costs. Builders have more flexibility on those than on list price.

### Contingencies

Include financing, inspection, appraisal, and title contingencies. In Knoxville's market, there's rarely competitive pressure that justifies waiving them outside of specific multiple-offer situations. For what to watch in the purchase agreement itself, see our post on [purchase agreement red flags Tennessee buyers often miss](/resources/purchase-agreement-red-flags).

Earnest money in Knox County typically runs 1%–2% of the purchase price. On a $301,000 home, that's $3,010–$6,020.<sup>3</sup>

## Step 5: Knoxville Closing Costs

On a $301,000 Knox County purchase, closing costs typically run $6,000–$15,050 (2%–5% of purchase price).<sup>9</sup>

### Tennessee State Taxes (Fixed)

- **Realty transfer tax**: $3.70 per $1,000. On $301,000: $1,114.
- **Mortgage recordation tax**: $1.15 per $1,000 of loan amount (first $2,000 exempt). On a $270,900 loan (10% down): $309.

These are fixed by TCA 67-4-409 and cannot be negotiated.<sup>10</sup>

### Knox County Property Taxes

Tennessee residential properties are assessed at 25% of appraised value. Knox County's property tax rate varies by municipality. The base Knox County rate is approximately $2.12 per $100 of assessed value for properties in unincorporated Knox County, with Knoxville city and other municipalities adding their own levies.<sup>11</sup>

On a $301,000 home assessed at $75,250:
- Unincorporated Knox County: approximately $1,595/year
- City of Knoxville residents pay additional city tax: verify current combined rate with the Knox County Trustee

Farragut has its own municipal tax rate. Verify the specific rate for any property within Farragut's boundaries before finalizing your budget.

### Lender and Title Fees

Shop at least three lenders. Origination fees, underwriting fees, and rate pricing vary enough that comparing Loan Estimates line by line can save $2,000–$4,000 on a $301,000 purchase.

For the complete fee breakdown and which ones are negotiable, see our [Tennessee closing costs guide](/resources/tennessee-closing-costs-fees-guide).

## Step 6: Down Payment Assistance in Knoxville

### THDA Great Choice Home Loan

Tennessee's statewide first-time buyer program offers up to 6% down payment and closing cost assistance (maximum $15,000) through the Great Choice Plus add-on. Minimum 640 credit score, income and purchase price limits apply.<sup>12</sup>

For Knox County, check current income limits at thda.org. The program has served a significant number of Knoxville-area buyers and is the most accessible statewide option.

### Knoxville's Community Development Programs

The City of Knoxville Community Development department periodically offers homebuyer assistance programs for buyers purchasing within city limits. These vary by funding availability. Contact the Knoxville Community Development Corporation for current program details.

### ORNL Federal Credit Union and Local Lenders

The Oak Ridge-Knoxville corridor has strong local credit unions (ORNL Federal Credit Union, TVA Community Credit Union) that offer competitive mortgage products for first-time buyers. Membership requirements are broader than the name suggests. Worth checking even if you don't work at ORNL or TVA.

For a full overview of loan programs available statewide, see our post on [Tennessee loan programs for first-time buyers](/resources/tennessee-loan-programs-2026).
