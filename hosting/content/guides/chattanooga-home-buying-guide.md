Chattanooga has spent the last decade transforming from an overlooked industrial city into one of the Southeast's most desirable mid-sized markets. The combination of outdoor recreation, a growing tech sector, a revitalized downtown, and home prices still below Nashville has drawn consistent demand from both local buyers and relocators. The median home price in Hamilton County sits around $310,000, and at that price, a traditional buyer's agent commission averages $7,502.<sup>1</sup><sup>2</sup>

That $7,500 is one of the easiest savings available to a Chattanooga buyer who's willing to handle the process themselves, with the right support. This guide covers everything you need: the neighborhoods, the market dynamics, Hamilton County's specific closing cost structure, and the step-by-step process for buying unrepresented in Chattanooga.

## Chattanooga's Housing Market in 2026

Chattanooga's market has stabilized from its post-pandemic surge but remains active. The city's draw for remote workers, outdoor enthusiasts, and retirees keeps demand above what pure local population growth would suggest.

Key current conditions:<sup>1</sup><sup>3</sup>

- Median sale price in Hamilton County: approximately $310,000
- Days on market: 35–65 days on average
- Inventory: improving across most price ranges, more balanced than the 2021–2023 period
- Price trends: appreciating modestly, not surging

The Chattanooga market rewards buyers who understand neighborhood-level dynamics. A home on the North Shore and a comparable home in East Ridge can behave very differently. Knowing which neighborhoods move quickly and which offer negotiating room is the most important research you can do before you start making offers.

## Chattanooga Neighborhoods: A Buyer's Orientation

Chattanooga has a distinct geography shaped by the Tennessee River, Lookout Mountain, Signal Mountain, and Missionary Ridge. Each creates a different buying environment.

### Downtown and River Districts

- **North Shore**: Across the Tennessee River from downtown, connected by the Walnut Street Bridge. Highly walkable, restaurant-dense, strong demand. Prices typically $350,000–$600,000+ for single-family homes. Condos and townhomes start lower.
- **Southside**: South of downtown, industrial-turned-residential character. Growing arts scene, active development. Mix of renovated warehouses and new construction.
- **Downtown Condos**: Limited single-family inventory, mostly condos and loft conversions. High price per square foot.

### Mountain and Ridge Communities

- **Signal Mountain**: Suburban municipality north of Chattanooga on Signal Mountain. Highly rated schools, outdoor access, larger lots. Entry-level around $400,000, significant inventory above $600,000.
- **Lookout Mountain**: Straddles the Tennessee-Georgia border. Small community with limited inventory and high price points.
- **Missionary Ridge**: East of downtown, historic ridge with views. Mix of price points, from $250,000 starter homes to $700,000+ properties with views.

### Established Suburbs

- **East Brainerd / Hamilton Place area**: Southeast Hamilton County, suburban and residential. Strong school options, newer construction. Prices $270,000–$450,000.
- **Ooltewah**: One of Hamilton County's fastest-growing areas. New construction, expanding commercial base, longer commutes to downtown. Prices $280,000–$500,000 for new construction.
- **Hixson**: North Chattanooga, established suburb. Good schools, access to Chester Frost Park and Lake Chickamauga. Prices $250,000–$450,000.

### More Affordable Areas

- **East Ridge**: City directly adjacent to Chattanooga on the southeast, technically a separate municipality. More affordable than Chattanooga proper, mixed housing stock, shorter commutes. Prices $180,000–$320,000.
- **Red Bank**: North of Chattanooga, separate municipality. Affordable entry points, older housing stock, renovation opportunities. Prices $175,000–$300,000.
- **Soddy-Daisy**: North Hamilton County. Rural-suburban character, Lake Chickamauga access, affordable. Prices $200,000–$350,000.

## Step 1: Build Your Support Team

### Hire a Chattanooga Real Estate Attorney

Tennessee doesn't require an attorney at closing, but for unrepresented buyers, hiring one is the single most important protective step you can take.<sup>5</sup> Chattanooga real estate attorneys typically charge $600–$1,200 for standard residential closing representation.

What they handle: contract language review, contingency terms, and closing representation. What they don't provide: neighborhood expertise, market pricing guidance, or showing coordination. Those are on you.

### Get Pre-Approved With a Lender

Get quotes from at least three lenders and compare Loan Estimates line by line before you start making offers. Chattanooga has several strong local lenders and credit unions alongside the national options, and rate and fee differences can be substantial.

Ask each lender specifically about seller concession limits for your loan type. Seller concessions are a normal negotiating tool in Chattanooga's market. Our [seller concessions guide](/resources/seller-concessions-tennessee-guide) explains how to structure and request them effectively.

### Line Up a Home Inspector

Standard inspections in Chattanooga run $325–$425. Book early, particularly in spring. Specific inspection considerations in Chattanooga:

- **Radon**: Hamilton County is in EPA Zone 1 for radon, the highest-risk designation. Always include radon testing with your inspection. Mitigation costs $800–$2,500 if levels exceed 4 pCi/L.<sup>6</sup>
- **Basement and crawlspace moisture**: Chattanooga's terrain creates drainage challenges on hillside and ridge properties. Moisture intrusion is common.
- **Foundation on sloped lots**: Mountain and ridge properties sit on challenging terrain. Foundation evaluation is especially important on hillside homes.
- **Septic systems**: Significant portions of Hamilton County, particularly outlying areas and mountain communities, use septic rather than sewer. Require a separate septic inspection and confirm the system's age, condition, and capacity.
- **Older electrical**: Chattanooga has significant pre-1960s housing stock. Dated wiring in older neighborhoods warrants an electrician's evaluation.

For a broader look at Tennessee inspection issues, see our post on [Tennessee home inspection issues that derail deals](/resources/tennessee-home-inspection-issues).

## Step 2: Search Chattanooga's Market

Zillow, Redfin, and Realtor.com display Chattanooga MLS listings in real time. The Greater Chattanooga Realtors MLS covers Hamilton County and surrounding areas including Bradley County (Cleveland), Catoosa County (Georgia), and others.

**One important note for Chattanooga buyers**: the metro area straddles the Tennessee-Georgia border. If you're considering homes in Catoosa County (Ringgold, Fort Oglethorpe) or Walker County, Georgia, you're dealing with Georgia law, Georgia closing costs, and a different purchase process. Make sure your attorney is licensed in the relevant state.

For showings, contact listing agents directly and identify yourself as an unrepresented buyer. Tennessee law (TCA 62-13-405) requires the listing agent to disclose their role and confirm it in writing before preparing any offer on your behalf.<sup>7</sup>

## Step 3: Research Comparable Sales

Chattanooga's geographic variation, river access, mountain views, and proximity to downtown all create significant price variation that requires careful comp matching. Use the Hamilton County Assessor's website for tax assessment history and prior sale records.<sup>8</sup>

Match comps on:
- Within 0.5 miles and the same general area (ridge, valley, suburban, etc.)
- Within 15–20% of square footage
- Same bedroom and bathroom count
- Comparable age, condition, and lot type

Homes on Signal Mountain and North Shore move differently than homes in Hixson and East Ridge. Don't use cross-neighborhood comps without adjusting for location premium.

## Step 4: Make an Offer in Chattanooga

### Strategy by Neighborhood

- **North Shore and popular urban areas**: Move within 1–2 days on well-priced homes. These properties can still attract multiple offers. Keep contingencies but be realistic about timelines.
- **Signal Mountain and premium suburbs**: More negotiating room. Longer days on market, higher price points, fewer buyers in the pool.
- **East Ridge, Red Bank, Soddy-Daisy**: Slower-moving market, more room to negotiate. Offers at 2%–5% below list are reasonable starting points on homes that have been active 45+ days.
- **New construction in Ooltewah and East Brainerd**: Negotiate on upgrades, rate buydowns, and closing costs rather than list price. Builders have more flexibility there.

Include financing, inspection, appraisal, and title contingencies on every offer. The current market doesn't justify waiving them. For what to watch in the purchase agreement itself, see our post on [purchase agreement red flags Tennessee buyers often miss](/resources/purchase-agreement-red-flags).

Earnest money in Hamilton County typically runs 1%–2% of the purchase price. On a $310,000 home, that's $3,100–$6,200.<sup>3</sup>

## Step 5: Chattanooga Closing Costs

On a $310,000 Hamilton County purchase, closing costs typically run $6,200–$15,500 (2%–5% of purchase price).<sup>9</sup>

### Tennessee State Taxes (Fixed)

- **Realty transfer tax**: $3.70 per $1,000. On $310,000: $1,147.
- **Mortgage recordation tax**: $1.15 per $1,000 of loan amount (first $2,000 exempt). On a $279,000 loan (10% down): $318.

These are set by TCA 67-4-409 and cannot be negotiated or shifted to the seller.<sup>10</sup>

### Hamilton County Property Taxes

Tennessee residential properties are assessed at 25% of appraised value. Hamilton County's combined property tax rate (county plus city of Chattanooga) is approximately $2.2394 per $100 of assessed value for properties within Chattanooga city limits, with the county-only rate lower for unincorporated areas.<sup>11</sup>

On a $310,000 home assessed at $77,500:
- City of Chattanooga rate: approximately $1,735/year
- Unincorporated Hamilton County: approximately $1,120/year

Signal Mountain, East Ridge, Red Bank, and other municipalities have their own tax rates. Always verify the specific rate for the municipality where the property is located before finalizing your carrying cost budget.

**Georgia border note**: If you're buying in Catoosa or Walker County in Georgia, property tax rates, assessment methods, and closing costs are entirely different from Tennessee. Work with a Georgia-licensed attorney for purchases across the state line.

### Lender and Title Fees

Shopping lenders is the highest-leverage action you can take on Chattanooga closing costs. Origination fees and underwriting fees vary significantly, and on a $310,000 purchase, the difference between the best and worst lender you talk to can easily be $2,500–$4,000.

## Step 6: Down Payment Assistance in Chattanooga

### THDA Great Choice Home Loan

Tennessee's statewide first-time buyer program offers up to 6% down payment and closing cost assistance (maximum $15,000) through the Great Choice Plus add-on. Minimum 640 credit score, with income and purchase price limits that vary by county.<sup>12</sup>

For Hamilton County, check current limits at thda.org. The program is widely used in the Chattanooga market and is the most accessible statewide option for qualifying buyers.

### City of Chattanooga Housing Programs

The City of Chattanooga's Department of Economic and Community Development administers homebuyer assistance programs periodically. These vary by funding cycle and eligibility requirements. Contact the department directly for current program availability.

### Tennessee Valley Authority Area Credit Unions

The TVA-region credit unions (Tennessee Valley Federal Credit Union, Chattanooga Federal Employees Credit Union) offer competitive mortgage products with first-time buyer programs. Membership eligibility is often broader than the name suggests.

For a full overview of statewide loan programs, see our post on [Tennessee loan programs for first-time buyers](/resources/tennessee-loan-programs-2026).
