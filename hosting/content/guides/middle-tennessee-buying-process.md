Every home purchase in Middle Tennessee moves through roughly the same ten stages, whether you hire a full-service buyer's agent, go it alone, or use BuyUnrepped's flat-fee structure. Knowing the map in advance is what separates a buyer who feels in control from one who's constantly caught off guard.

## 1. Clarify budget and financing

Talk to a local lender for pre-approval so you know your price range, your monthly payment comfort zone, and any conditions that could affect timing. Cash buyers should still map out liquidity and closing costs before they start touring. A lender who understands Middle Tennessee files and answers their phone will save you real time later — see [our lender picks](/vendors) if you need a place to start.

## 2. Search and tour homes

Many unrepresented buyers use listing agents for property access and questions about specific homes. Keep notes on condition, HOA rules, and anything that could affect value or your offer strategy. Our [Search & tour playbook](/playbook) has scripts, etiquette, and a step-by-step flow built specifically for unrepresented buyers — including how to actually reach the listing agent on Zillow, Homes.com, and Redfin instead of a paid-lead agent.

## 3. Offer strategy and contract package

When you're ready to write an offer, comps, contingencies, and Tennessee contract language all matter. This is where BuyUnrepped's [$995 Offer Package](/pricing) fits: a ready-to-submit offer, a strategy consult, a CMA and broker price opinion, and Tennessee-approved forms — without acting as your buyer's agent.

## 4. Negotiation and mutual acceptance

Counteroffers, deadlines, and seller responses move quickly. You communicate directly with the listing side; we help you stay organized and clear on what each version of the contract actually means before you sign it.

## 5. Earnest money and escrow

Once terms are accepted, you'll deposit earnest money per the contract. Know who holds it, how it's applied at closing, and what happens to it if a contingency is exercised.

## 6. Inspection and due diligence window

Use your inspection period to understand the property's condition, negotiate repairs if appropriate, and satisfy any other contract contingencies before they expire. This window has hard deadlines — missing one can mean losing your right to negotiate or walk away.

## 7. Appraisal and loan underwriting

Your lender orders an appraisal to confirm value. Underwriting may request updated documents during this stage — respond quickly so financing stays on track and your closing date doesn't slip.

## 8. Title and insurance

The title company clears ownership and schedules closing. You'll review a closing disclosure and need to arrange homeowner's insurance effective on closing day. See [our title company picks](/vendors) for teams that communicate clearly and run a tight file.

## 9. Final walk-through

Confirm the home's condition matches your expectations and that any agreed-upon repairs are actually complete before you sign closing documents. This is your last check before you're financially committed.
