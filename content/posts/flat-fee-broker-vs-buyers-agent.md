The NAR settlement was supposed to change the math on buyer's agent commissions. It didn't. Buyer-agent fees rebounded to pre-settlement levels within three quarters of the new rules taking effect, and commissions actually increased in 39 states through 2025.<sup>4</sup><sup>5</sup>

What the settlement *did* do is force a number that used to be invisible into plain sight. At Tennessee's average rate of 2.92%, a buyer's agent on a Nashville median-priced home (~$476K) costs approximately **$13,899**.<sup>1</sup><sup>8</sup> Once you see that number, it's hard to unsee it.

So the question becomes: is a traditional buyer's agent always worth that cost, and what exactly is the alternative? For a deeper look at [what the NAR settlement actually changed](/resources/nar-settlement-home-buyers-2026), we've covered that elsewhere. This post focuses on a specific comparison: what you get with a traditional buyer's agent versus a flat-fee support model, and when each one makes sense for Tennessee buyers.

Here are the 8 differences that matter most.

## The Landscape Has Changed, Here's Where Things Stand

Before getting into the differences, a quick picture of where the market is today.

The 2024 NAR settlement required written buyer agreements before touring homes and removed commission offers from the MLS. Neither change lowered commissions. The national average buyer-agent commission is back to roughly 2.75%, and Tennessee buyers are paying above that, the state's average buyer's agent commission is **2.92%**, with total commissions averaging **6.05%** versus a national average of 5.57%.<sup>1</sup><sup>4</sup><sup>5</sup>

There's a Tennessee-specific wrinkle that makes this more consequential than in most states: **commission rebates are illegal here**. In most states, some discount brokerages will rebate a portion of their commission back to buyers as cash or closing cost credits. Tennessee prohibits that, it's one of only ~10 states with this restriction.<sup>10</sup> If you hire a buyer's agent in Tennessee, you pay the full negotiated rate. There's no rebate path, no workaround.

That means your choices are: pay a traditional agent, negotiate a lower rate upfront, use a flat-fee model, or go entirely without representation. For more on [how commission rates break down in dollar terms](/resources/buyer-agent-commissions-2026), see our commission guide.

## What Each Model Actually Is

It helps to be precise about what you're comparing before diving into differences.

### Traditional Buyer's Agent

A traditional buyer's agent is a licensed REALTOR who enters a formal agency relationship with you through a written buyer representation agreement, either an Exclusive (RF142) or Non-Exclusive (RF144) agreement under Tennessee REALTORS forms.<sup>2</sup> Once that agreement is signed, the agent owes you full fiduciary duties under Tennessee Code Title 62: loyalty, obedience, diligence, disclosure, confidentiality, and accountability.<sup>9</sup> Tennessee law describes these as "the highest duties known to the law."

The agent is paid via commission, typically 2.5–3% of the purchase price in Tennessee, either by the seller as a concession or directly by you at closing. For a detailed breakdown of what you're signing, see our [buyer-broker agreement guide](/resources/buyer-broker-agreements-explained).

### Flat-Fee Broker / Non-Representational Support

A flat-fee support provider is a licensed brokerage that provides structured guidance, tools, and documents, without entering a buyer agency relationship. Under Tennessee law (Title 62), this role is a facilitator or transaction broker: a non-agent who may assist both parties but is not the advocate or representative of either.<sup>9</sup>

The provider is paid a fixed upfront fee regardless of the home's purchase price. The buyer handles direct communication with the listing agent; the flat-fee provider works behind the scenes.

### Difference 2: Fiduciary Duty

This is the most significant legal difference, and it's worth understanding clearly.

A traditional buyer's agent owes you six fiduciary duties under Tennessee Code Title 62: **loyalty, obedience, diligence, disclosure, confidentiality, and accountability**. These aren't just professional standards, they're legal obligations, described in Tennessee law as "the highest duties known to the law."<sup>9</sup>

Critically, these duties only attach when a written bilateral agency agreement is in place. Without a signed buyer representation agreement, there is no fiduciary relationship, period.<sup>9</sup><sup>2</sup>

A flat-fee non-representational provider operates as a facilitator under Tennessee law. All licensed Tennessee real estate professionals owe a baseline duty to treat all parties honestly and fairly. But the full fiduciary protection, an agent who is legally bound to put your interests first in every decision, belongs only to buyers who have a signed agency agreement.

The practical implication: with a traditional agent, the listing agent on the other side knows you have someone in your corner. Without one, you're advocating for yourself.

### Difference 3: Who Talks to the Listing Agent

A traditional buyer's agent acts as a buffer and advocate. They communicate directly with the listing agent, control what information you share, and handle the negotiation, including how much enthusiasm to show, what terms to push for, and how to respond to counteroffers without tipping your hand.

With flat-fee support, **you** are the person communicating with the listing agent. The flat-fee provider prepares you with offer strategy, smart scripts, and guidance, but they aren't in that conversation with you.

BuyUnrepped is explicit about this: the platform provides "structure, preparation, and strategic guidance behind the scenes, while you maintain control of conversations, negotiations, and decisions."

One thing that doesn't change either way: [the listing agent represents the seller](/resources/buying-home-without-buyers-agent-guide), not you. Whether you show up with a buyer's agent or alone, the listing agent's fiduciary duty runs to their client. Don't rely on them for advice about whether to make an offer, how much to offer, or what repairs to request.

### Difference 5: Incentive Alignment

A traditional buyer's agent is paid a percentage of what you pay for the house. That creates a structural tension: the higher your purchase price, the higher their commission. An agent who advises you to offer $15,000 less on a home earns less than one who counsels a full-price offer.

Most buyer's agents act with integrity despite this structure. But the incentive misalignment is real and worth acknowledging.

A flat fee eliminates it. BuyUnrepped's fee doesn't change based on what you pay for the home. There's no financial incentive to push you toward a higher price, a faster deal, or a larger purchase.<sup>7</sup>

This isn't an accusation, it's a structural observation. When evaluating any professional relationship, understanding how the advisor gets paid is a reasonable starting point.

### Difference 6: What Happens When Things Get Complicated

This is where the difference matters most in practice.

A traditional buyer's agent has typically navigated dozens or hundreds of transactions. When an inspection uncovers a $20,000 foundation issue, they know how to frame the repair request, when to walk away, and how to negotiate a price reduction that reflects the actual cost. When an appraisal comes in low, they know the options: challenge the appraisal, renegotiate the price, or structure a concession. When a seller pushes back on contingencies, they manage that conversation so you don't have to.

With flat-fee support at the basic tier, complications land on you. You're managing the inspection response, the appraisal gap, the financing snag, with whatever research and guidance you can find.

BuyUnrepped's Full Strategy Pass closes a significant portion of this gap. The tier includes licensed REALTOR on-call support specifically for navigating complications, along with inspection and appraisal guidance built into the transaction coordination. For situations that reach legal complexity, a real estate attorney ($750–$1,250 flat fee) adds another layer of protection.

The biggest practical risk for unrepresented buyers: missing [inspection contingency deadlines](/resources/buying-home-without-buyers-agent-guide). In Tennessee, earnest money typically runs 1–3% of the purchase price, on a $400K home, that's $4,000–$12,000 sitting in escrow. Miss a contingency deadline and that money is potentially at risk. Deadline tracking isn't glamorous, but it's where the real exposure lives.<sup>11</sup>

### Difference 7: When Each Makes Sense

Neither model is universally right. Here's a practical breakdown.

**A traditional buyer's agent likely makes sense if:**

- You're a first-time buyer who hasn't navigated contracts, contingencies, or closing timelines before
- You're buying in a highly competitive multi-offer market where professional negotiation experience matters
- The home has complex issues, title problems, zoning questions, unusual property conditions
- You don't have the time to manage the process yourself (this takes real hours)
- You're buying a short sale, new construction, bank-owned property, or foreclosure<sup>7</sup>

**Flat-fee support likely makes sense if:**

- You've purchased a home before and have a working understanding of the process
- You're buying at a higher price point, where the commission savings are most substantial
- You're comfortable communicating directly with listing agents and doing your own research
- You have the time and attention to manage your own deadlines and coordinate your transaction
- You want professional tools, forms, and guidance without committing to a percentage-based fee<sup>6</sup>

The honest version: experienced buyers who know what they're doing and are willing to be their own project managers are strong candidates for flat-fee support. First-time buyers facing their first purchase agreement in a competitive market are strong candidates for traditional representation.

### Difference 8: The Tennessee-Specific Wrinkle, No Rebates

This difference is unique to Tennessee and changes the math in a meaningful way.

In most states, some discount brokerages offer buyers a rebate, they collect the full buyer-agent commission from the seller and return a portion of it to the buyer as cash or closing cost credits. It's a way to get some representation and some savings.

Tennessee bans this. Under a law signed by Governor Bredesen and reinstated by TREC in 2007, agents cannot give cash rebates or directly pay for a buyer's closing costs. Tennessee is one of only ~10 states with this restriction still in place.<sup>10</sup>

What that means in practice: you can't hire an agent and get money back. The full commission is the full commission. For more on how [commission rebates work (or don't) in Tennessee](/resources/buyer-agent-commissions-2026), see our commission breakdown.

In most states, a middle path exists, hire an agent, pay a partial fee, get a rebate on the rest. In Tennessee, that path is closed. The choice here is more binary than almost anywhere else: pay the traditional commission in full, negotiate a lower rate upfront, use a flat-fee model, or go without.

## What Buyers Give Up Going Flat-Fee, and How to Fill the Gaps

Being honest about this matters. Flat-fee support isn't a perfect substitute for traditional representation. Here are the three most significant gaps, and how to close them.

**Gap 1: Legal contract review.** A buyer's agent doesn't provide legal advice, but they do flag issues in purchase agreements and help you understand what you're signing. Without that, your contracts need review from someone. The solution: hire a Tennessee real estate attorney for closing. Tennessee doesn't require an attorney, but for unrepresented buyers it's close to essential. Expect to pay $750–$1,250 as a flat fee for closing services, or $150–$350/hour for more complex situations.<sup>9</sup>

**Gap 2: Comparable sales and pricing guidance.** A buyer's agent provides a formal CMA. You'll need to build your own. BuyUnrepped includes market data access, supplement that with county assessor records and the "recently sold" filters on Redfin and Zillow. Focus on closed sales within the past six months, within half a mile, with similar size and condition.

**Gap 3: Inspection and deadline management.** This is where unrepresented buyers are most exposed. The Full Strategy Pass addresses it directly, deadline tracking, reminders, and on-call REALTOR support are all included. If you're using the basic Ready to Submit Offer tier, have your attorney calendar every contingency deadline on day one.<sup>11</sup>

Total cost of filling the gaps properly: $750–$1,250 for an attorney plus $3,595 for BuyUnrepped's Full Strategy Pass comes to roughly **$4,345–$4,845**. That's still less than a 2.92% buyer's agent commission on any Tennessee home priced above $166,000. On a $476K Nashville home, you'd save approximately **$9,000** even after filling every gap.<sup>1</sup>
